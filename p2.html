Biomimicry in cities

City Development

bird trains

Biomimicry is the method of imitating aspects of the biological world in technology and design to solve human problems. One area where biomimicry can be found is in city development. In fact, there are numerous instances where biomimicry has been used to further transportation and architecture. Biomimicry in the case of transportation allows people aerodynamic traversal across vast distances. Take for instance how in Japan, bullet trains have been partly designed off of the beak of kingfisher birds. To further explain, prior to the introduction of kingfisher biology in locomotion, Japanese bullet trains faced a problem involving their nose, which would compress air into a soundwave, eventually expanding into a sonic boom out the end of the tunnel, disturbing nearby people, passengers, and wildlife, as well slowing down the trains, due to the compressed air building a barrier of sorts in front the train as it moved. However, later on, the Japanese Railway Company’s chief engineer took notice of kingfisher birds and their long and narrow beaks. Such beaks allow these birds the advantage of having water pass around their beak than build in front of it. Essentially, with their beaks, kingfisher birds could dive into the water with little splash. Thus, bullet trains in Japan started to have their noses more long and narrowed, like that of a kingfisher’s beak, as such a shape would allow air to flow over the trains and their nose rather than become compressed, making the bullet trains less loud and faster.

Termite aircon

Biomimicry has also fostered the advancement in architecture as previously mentioned. This is found in Harare, the capital of Zimbabwe, where a building called the Eastgate Centre stands, which like many other buildings in the city, could get really hot at times. Not to mention, air conditioning could be costly as well. This led to termite mounds being looked at by Zimbabwean architect Mike Pearce as a model for the building, as termite mounds had a form of makeshift air conditioning, allowing for cool air to remain at the bottom, while the warm air would be circulated at the top through tiny holes. Likewise, when Eastgate Centre incorporated Pearce’s ideas of imitating the design of a termite mound, the building ended up with fans at the bottom of each of its towers, pushing cool air from the bottom upward through ducts, where warm air is carried out through chimneys. The inspiration from termite mounds have actually worked out in the Eastgate centre’s favor, allowing it to use 10% less energy than other buildings its size, and allowing it to save almost 3.5 million dollars on air conditioning.
