background

In the 2008 Olympic Games, athletes in the same fascinating swimsuit took 98% of medals in swimming, astonishing the world. Less than 2 years later, however, the very swimsuit that athletes wore, the LZR Racer made by Speedo, had been banned from the Olympic, dropping all records it contributed to making. The main reason under this decision was, ironically, its excessive effectiveness originated from a high-technology which mimicked the mechanism of shark skin. Then, how could the Speedo team have made this overperforming swimsuit?

Mechanisms

When an object swims, frictions generated from different properties of the object oppose the way it tries to go to in the fluid. Many researchers found that, cleverly, sharks reduce the skin friction drag by covering their skin with denticles. The denticles are small scales on the skin of sharks which look similar to the tooth. The roughness of the skin from the denticles leads to an easy control of the water flow, thereby making sharks swim faster.

Application

Applying this mechanism and acknowledging that around 25% of the opposing force in swimming is derived from the viscous drag, Speedo helped by NASA tested 60 different fabrics to determine the appropriate material for a swimsuit. With the adopted fabric which causes more resistance to the water with less amount of weight, Speedo also added a feature in which the location of zippers is lower than previous swimwears and the swimsuit bonds the body of swimmers entirely. As a result, Speedo showed the best swimsuit in the world which contributed to 6 percent smaller drag than usual with lightweight and extremely enhancing the speed of medal-winners in the Olympics, which is the LZR Racer.

What is biomimicry?

Biomimicry is the method of imitating aspects of the biological world in technology and design to solve human problems.
